Style


UX Patterns · Web Development
Duration
6 Months
Role
UX Engineer
Tools
ReactJs, HTML, CSS, SVG, Google Analytics, Sketch
Overview
Style is a design system that defines the interaction standards, design patterns, and guidelines for all digital channels in ZALORA. It was my job to provide aid from the development perspective and creatively direct the creation and documentation of the design system. I worked with Pramiti (UX Manager, Interaction), Eugene (UX Manager, Research), Sheila (UI Designer) and, Doro (Product Manager) on the project.
Deliverables
  • ZALORA/Style (CSS component library)
  • Style-react (React component library)
  • Documentation Portal

A fundamental struggle that has been plaguing ZALORA is the disjunction of its products and lack of cohesive system. Due to ZALORA’s organisation and its organic growth, teams have been historically working in silos, with each team creating their own UI, logic and UX patterns with little consensus and guidance holistically.

Style is aimed to provide a joyful, enriching, unified experience to the customers, as well as to ensure a low cost of changes and a balance between creativity and efficiency. Style isn’t just UI style guides, but also the way the team works and what are the team values and principles.

The Process

Strategy

Stakeholder Meetings

Getting everyone on board at the beginning, and collaborating with them throughout the process is essential for the success of the project. Thus, before starting, I talked to the major stakeholders who might need to reference or update the system to understand what works well and what challenges could be solved.

Foundations

Setting the foundations right before moving to the visual side is also important as it will speed up the later stage.

Firstly, I worked with the design team to settle down:

Tools
Naming

To ensure effective communication between different teams, we followed some best naming rules used by the development team. For versioning, we followed the semantic versioning. We also decided to name the components by their functionalities to guide and constraint the usage of the components, e.g. label--error instead of label--red.

Documentation

We decided to keep all the documentation, research assets in google docs. As it's easily accessible by all teams, plus the team can provide inline and timely feedback.

UI Inventory

A UI inventory helped us easily identify problems and uncover information we didn’t know existed. It helped us to understand what components were used in the system repeatedly, and also accelerated the progress when we want to apply the new unified design to the products in the future stage.

Design, Code & Doc

We delivered the components/elements progressively over sprints. We started by designing and building the visual style (e.g., color, typography, and space), which was followed by UI components (e.g., Button, Tabs, Labels).

Data-driven Design

I looked for tracking data in Google Analytics that can be used to inform the design decisions. Based on the traffic from different platforms, we decided to adopt a mobile-first approach. We defined the breakpoints for the grid system by referencing the distribution of the screen sizes.

While designing, we followed some best practices to ensure the accessibility of the product. E.g. We used WCAG 2.0 guidelines for choosing and evaluating our color combinations, we used system font to ensure a smooth and native experience to the customers.

Implementation

Due to the current implementation of the mobile website and the desktop website, we implemented two libraries: ZALORA/Style and ZALORA/Styleguide.

ZALORA/Style

This library was built by CSS and HTML. It defined the appearance of components and the animations.

ZALORA/Style-react

This library defined the look of the components by referencing ZALORA/Style. It also defined the behaviors assisted by ReactJs. It mainly served for the mobile platform.

Documentation

There were two kinds of documentation being provided: the design guidelines, and the distribution guides.

Design Guidelines

The design guidelines include the general guide (e.g., principles of ZALORA product design, ZALORA's tone of voice that governed the whole design system), as well as the docs for individual components (e.g., the spec, usage in code and design, and suggested/anti-patterns).

Distribution Guides

The distribution guide is for developers who want to distribute to the library. It includes the library structure, conventions, tooling, etc.

Workshops & Talks

In order to get more people to use our new design system, we held workshops and talks to market our design system. Our participants were not limited to developers, PMs, and designers. We also invited the QAs and teams from marketing department.


Apart from holding workshops and talks to introduce the design system, as a developer, I also implemented a playground as part of the documentation portal to encourage people to try the new design system without bothering setting up the development environment.

Positive Results

The design system has had a positive impact on the website performance and development efficiency, at the time of writing (1 months since v1 launch).


  • The first meaningful paint of the mobile web homepage reduced almost 1s.
  • The bounce rate of the mobile web homepage reduced 30%.
  • The time of developing the frontend for the desktop and mobile websites reduced ~50% (estimated by the developers)
